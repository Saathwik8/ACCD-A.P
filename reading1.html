We define a prototype as any representation of a design idea, regardless of medium

I found this quote interesting as it offers an operational definition of what a prototype is. Especially when it mentions "regardless of the medium'', it makes the application of the definition flexible. Usually, prototypes are used to test and understand systems before being released to customers. Thus the capability of prototypes to be medium-independent makes them very powerful as tools. Simple materials are used to provide an understanding of the object of testing.




❝

A prototype may explore questions or design options in one, two or all three dimensions of the model

Prototypes being an open ended concept allows for a very interesting process. However the dimension and the quality of the prototype enables more comprehensive test results. If a product needs to be tested for specific outcomes, the kind of prototype really matters, E.g., while testing how a material texture feels, the surface of the prototype also must be using the materials as the main product and should also replicate the products dimensions. Sometimes It becomes imperative to use the correct design, form, material and other test parameters to be representative of the final product.




❝

Making separate prototypes enabled specific design questions to be addressed with as much clarity as possible

This article addresses this while explaining Example 3 that explored programming environments that best supported the display of true 3D perspectives during manipulation. This quote indicates that each prototype is designed to test a specific hypothesis. And it is interesting how prototypes different in form, shape, ordination can test aspects of a design and those learnings enable re-iterations that lead to the final refined form. This final form itself can have a completely different design than the prototypes. Though this is a well understood concept, it is less spoken about, and the article mentioning it seemed very interesting.
