However thoroughly sensors might be deployed in a city, they’ll only ever capture the qualities about the world that are amenable to capture. As the architect and critical-data scholar Laura Kurgan has argued, “we measure the things that are easy to measure ... the things that are cheap to measure,”33 and this suggests that sensors, however widely deployed, will only ever yield a partial picture of the world.

I had personally worked on a city planning project back in 2016 and a major issue we faced was to understand what kinds of metrics must we be measuring in order to increase the efficiency of a system. It is interesting to notice that the article also says that we only measure the variables that are immediately Abe to access and not actually what are needed to develop insight. This primary leads into understanding what is to be measured, how to generate insight ad how can we as designers create systems that generate insights and no just represent data that is normally collected. How does one create representations from simple logging that enable individuals to derive insight. In both, the leanings we had and the systems mentioned in the article, it’s not about relying sensors and having a lot of system that capture the data but it is how the data can be used for analysis and how that can help in improving systems. It also calls to how do we start to develop better sensors that give us more accurate and diverse sets of readings that can potentially give us further insights into systems.
