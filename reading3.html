But the main problem with the virtual assistant is that it fosters an approach to the world that is literally thoughtless, leaving users disinclined to sit out any particularly prolonged frustration of desire, and ever less critical about the processes that result in the satisfaction of their needs and wants.

Virtual assistants - though very helpful - in the longer run can cause unexpected harm. As mentioned “world that is literally thoughtless” is the primary fear that I also carry about them. As they enable the concept of instant information and instant gratification, the lack of it can equally cause frustration and anxiety which might be a scenario we need to consider in future design interactions. Slowly, humans will stop to process a few simple things by themselves and that level of support or dependency can stunt individual capability. Not training our minds in simple things can result in a lowered sense of awareness of the mind as well.

However thoroughly sensors might be deployed in a city, they’ll only ever capture the qualities about the world that are amenable to capture. As the architect and critical-data scholar Laura Kurgan has argued, “we measure the things that are easy to measure ... the things that are cheap to measure,”33 and this suggests that sensors, however widely deployed, will only ever yield a partial picture of the world.

I had personally worked on a city planning project back in 2016 and a major issue we faced was to understand what kinds of metrics must we be measuring in order to increase the efficiency of a system. It is interesting to notice that the article also says that we only measure the variables that are immediate in access. It mentions how we are not measuring parameters that can help us develop insight. This primary leads into understanding what is to be measured, how to generate insight and how we as designers should create visualizations that generate insights and not just represent data that is normally collected. How does one create representations from simple logging that enable individuals to derive insight. In both, the leanings I had and the systems mentioned in the article, it’s not about relying on sensors and having a lot of them that capture the data, but it is how the data can be used for analysis and how that can help in improving systems. It also calls on how we start to develop better sensors that give us more accurate and diverse sets of readings that can potentially give us further insights into systems.
